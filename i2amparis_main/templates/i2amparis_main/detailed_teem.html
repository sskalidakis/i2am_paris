The TEEM Suite model

Short overview

TEEM, the TEESlab Modeling suite, is an ensemble of high-resolution energy system simulation and optimization models consisting of:

      (i) the Business Strategy Assessment Model (BSAM), which is an agent-based electricity wholesale market simulation model which simulates the complex operations within a power pool central dispatch Day Ahead Market,
      (ii) the Agent-based Technology adOption Model (ATOM), which simulates the dynamics of technology adoption among consumers,
      (iii) the Dynamic high-Resolution dEmand-sidE Management (DREEM) model, which serves as an entry point in Demand-Side management modeling in the building sector, and
      (iv) the Adaptive polIcymaking Model (AIM), which generates real time visualizations of adaptive policy maps, showing various sequences of policy options leading robustly to desired policy outcomes.

The modules of TEEM can be coupled (i.e., soft- and/or hard- linking) with each other, as well as other models/modules, to support the evaluation of energy related challenges (e.g., the expected impacts from bringing demand-flexibility into the power market, the adoption of technologies that can be regarded as flexibility enablers, the technoeconimic impact of high RES shares, etc.). It can also perform meta-analysis of other models' inputs and outputs, to identify the conditions of success of a policy instrument, as well as generate robust policy pathways, by treating various modelling assumption as potential future evolutions of policies' context.

Key features of the TEEM Suite model

TEEM incorporates the following features:

1) Wholesale electricity market modelling (BSAM):

Modeling of the day-ahead market by solving the Security Constrained Unit Commitment and Economic Dispatch problems. System constraints (e.g., supply equals demand, satisfaction of reserves requirements), operating plants constraints (e.g., minimum and maximum generation, uptime and downtime requirements), system reserves, price caps, etc., are considered in simulations. Agents (e.g., thermal generators, imports and hydroelectric generators) within the wholesale electricity market are modelled as entities aiming in profit maximisation, while the market is cleared with objective function being the minimization of electricity generation costs. Thus BSAM simulates a competitive electricity market.

2) Technology adoption considering behavioural uncertainty (ATOM)

Simulation of technology adoption under policy schemes of interest, quantifing uncertainties related to agents’ preferences and decision-making criteria. The novelty of the model lies in obtaining realistic uncertainty bounds and splitting the output uncertainty in its major contributing sources. A calibration module defines the parameters influencing the agents’ behavior, and suitable ranges of values according to historical data and observations are defined. A sensitivity analysis module quantifies uncertainties related to potential adopters' attributes and decision-making criteria. Finally, a scenario analysis module, based on historical observations, simulates the plausible behavior of agents, within the investigated geographic and socioeconomic context, under technological support policy schemes of interest (i.e., forward-looking simulations).

3) Integrated demand-side management modelling (DREEM)

DREEM is a fully integrated dynamic high-resolution model resolving key features that are not found together in existing Demand-Side Management (DSM) models. The model serves as an entry point in DSM modeling in the building sector, by expanding the computational capabilities of existing Building Energy System (BES) models to assess the benefits and limitations of demand-flexibility, primarily for consumers, and for other power actors involved. DREEM is a hybrid bottom up model that combines the key features of both statistical and engineering models. The novelty of the DREEM model lies mainly in its modularity, as its structure is decomposed into individual modules characterized by the main principles of component- and modular-based system modeling approach, namely "the interdependence" of decisions within modules.

Hierarchical structure of the DREEM model

      - Weather-climate module: Responsible for generating climatic boundary conditions. It reads weather data from the respective files and then provides them to the other components, where and when necessary.
      - Building envelope module: Modelling of different building typologies with the corresponding characteristics, properties and heat conduction elements.
      - Electricity demand module: Calculation of the electricity demand considering the occupancy of the building, the electric appliances used, the heating, ventilation and airconditioning usage coupled with a smart thermostat, etc.
      - Thermal comfort module: Responsible for determining, based on international standards, appropriate thermal conditions and temperature ranges that result in thermal satisfaction of occupants.
      - Flexibility management module: Calculation of the solar generation based on roof tilt and locational irradiation, simulation of the operation of electricty storage, and, simulation of the control algorithm of a smart thermostat.
      - Demand-Response (DR) module: Simulation of DR mechanisms that motivate the consumers to respond to real time price signals.
      - Control strategies module: Responsible for the energy management supervision strategy that, given the time-shifting events of demand and the occupancy signals, aims at achieving energy savings and costeffectiveness.

4) Dynamic adaptive policy pathways generation for robust policy planning (AIM)

A decision support, data-driven, approach, facilitates robust decision making under deep uncertainties, and co-development with stakeholders of policy pathways, towards a desired target. This is done by applying a stepwise policy implementation and assessment approach, which considers the impact of policy implementation and uses it to inform the effectiveness of consequent policies. Using inputs and outputs of simulation models: (i) conditions under-, and the timeframe beyond-which a policy measure starts to deviate from set targets are investigatd, (ii) maps of diverse Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways are visualised, showing sequences of policies leading to a common policy target, under different perspectives regarding the objective (i.e., policy cost minimization, early target achievement, etc) until the final target, and (iii) a monitoring system enabling timely identification of influential contextual influences which might affect policy outcome is set up, enabling that way, dynamic adaptation during real world policy implementation. Modelling outcomes are presented via easily-undestandable visualizations, which feature quantitative results regarding the evaluation of policies' performence under contextual evolutions. With these visualizations, the bulk of data generated is reduced to only those of actual use of stakeholders, thus attracting their interest in using and being informed by the model.

Climate module & emissions granularity

The BSAM model of the TEEM Suite can calculate the CO2 emissions for electricity generation from thermal generating resources and the respective emissions cost considering the EU ETS emission prices.

Socioeconomic dimensions

Economic Growth (aspects of technology diffusion)

ATOM models the triggering of private investments, in the form of new capacity additions of solar PV technologies, capturing also benefits and profitability aspects for consumers. Behavioural aspects relevant to the decision-making criteria of consumers are also caputed by the model.

Building sector growth

In DREEM, demand for energy services in residential and commercial buildings is primarily increased by total building floor space, which is driven by GDP per capita, and saturates at a given maximum value, defined per region. Per unit of floor space, demand for energy services increases over time depending on the relative price of the service, and the difference between the base year saturation and the defined maximum saturation for a certain service. For heating and cooling services, the maximum saturation depends on regionally explicit heating and cooling degree days.

Energy Poverty

As most European countries have no official definition for the term "energy poverty", this state is often described as the "inability to keep homes adequately warm". DREEM can estimate the energy needs of a household taking into consideration details as occupancy and thermal comfort, simulating this way the household with accurate “real-world” conditions. Therefore, the results of DREEM combined with real demographic data can help users identify energy poor households.

Mitigation/adaptation measures and technologies

BSAM simulates both fossil fuelled (i.e., lignite and natural gas) and low-carbon (i.e., wind and PV) electricity generation technologies. Variables such as the EU-ETS carbon price and the cost of fossil fuels, as well as large renewables' penetration scenarios are modelled, to showcase their effect on the resulting electricity mix and system-wide CO2 emissions for electricity generation.

ATOM simulates the adoption rate of low-carbon technologies, such as solar PV and battery energy storage systems in the building sector.

DREEM simulates decentralized RES technologies, storage technologies, energy efficiency measures and practices, as well as, cooling/heating practices in buildings.

AIM is capable of performing meta-analysis of the inputs and outputs of simulation models, towards mitigation/adaptation targets. For instance, it has been used in soft-link with ATOM to generate dynamic adaptive policy pathways supporting the diffusion of small-scale PV installations towards the achievement of the 2030 capacity targets in Greece (sequence of incentive-based policies). This was done considering various evolutions of the Greek context, thus adding robustness to the generated policy pathways.

Economic rationale and model solution

BSAM is built upon the notion that agents aim to maximize their profits within a competitive market aiming to minimize the total electricity generation costs. Agents interact with each other within the wholesale electricity market by placing bids for their electricity generation. Agents' profits are calculated by considering a profit margin above marginal costs for electricity generation. Agent profit bids decisions are continuously updated based on knowledge and information acquired from previous bid decisions. An optimization of the Unit Commitment and Economic Dispatch problem minimizes the total electricity generation costs. To do so, a ranking of agent price bids in an increasing order is followed by an iterative process of clearing electricity-price bids under the constraint that supply should equal demand. After meeting the demand requirements the process stops and the SMP and the total system cost are calculated.

The core principle of ATOM is that of agent-based modelling. In particular, each agent has a private initial belief about the expected annual cash inflows from investing in a PV system. This belief is expressed as a Gaussian distribution. Furthermore, to capture the effects of social learning, each agent receives information from the agents in its social circle that have already invested in a PV system. This information concerns the actual profitability of their investments so far, and is used to update the agent’s beliefs. This is equivalent to updating a Gaussian prior, given a new observation. Accordingly, each agent has a social circle. This condition is modeled as a “small-world” network, which is a type of mathematical graph.

DREEM is a Demand-Side Management (DSM) model, generating energy demand in the building sector, for realistic building typologies of interest, aggragating at a higher scale based on survey/census data or historical observations. The model can quantify the value of demand-flexibility primarily for consumers, and then for other power actors involved, through the inclusion of a rich set of technology solutions (small-scale PV, battery storage, smart devices and control management systems, energy efficiency and renovation measues, etc.). By aggragating at e.g., national level, the model can derive pathways for the achievement of, for example, energy efficiency targets, and quantify the amount of public and/or private investments needed towards this end. For the Demand-Response compononent, a Reinforcement Learning-based algorithm is employed to find the optimal policy (demand response signals) that could maximize the central entity's assumed profits.

AIM with appropriate modifications, follows the economic rational of any model, to perform exploratory analysis of other models' simulation outcomes.

Policy questions and SDGs

Key policies that can be addressed

Emissions-related policies

With BSAM CO2 emissions reduction can be investigated through scenarios of EU-ETS carbon price evolution. The user can input scenarios of estimated EU-ETS carbon price, which is modelled as an additional cost on the marginal costs of fossil fuel generators (i.e., lignite- and gas-fueled plants). This additional cost increases the difficulty of high CO2 emitters to be competitive in the wholesale electricity market, altering their contribution to the electricity mix and the resulting system-wide CO2 emissions.

RES & storage support policies

ATOM can be used to investigate the expected effectiveness of different policy support schemes (e.g., Feed-in-Tarrifs, Net-metering, different typologies of self-consumption schemes that subsidize part of storage systems' initial investment costs, etc.), in driving investments in small-scale PV, towards the achievement of small-scale PV capacity targets for the geographical and socioeconomic context of interest.

DREEM can assess the electrical performance of small-scale PV installations. The complete possible monthly energy yield of the PV installation can be calculated during simulation and therefore the power generated can be computed. Thus, policy instruments that support solar PV installation (i.e., FiTs, Net-Metering, Self-Consumption, etc.) can be examined and further deployment opportunities can be identified.

DREEM simulates also storage technologies (e.g., batteries) quantifying benefits of storage installations in the building sector. Different support policies, as for example subsidizing storage's initial investment costs are considered by the model.

Demand-Response policies

Demand-Response (DR) is increasingly attracting policy attention and residential consumers could offer a substantial new source of demand-side flexibility. Demand-Response schemes offered to customers, are considered integral part of Demand-Side Management modeling. The DREEM model brings together all important aspects of end-use with a DR modeling framework, that builds on the concept of time-based DR methods. In particular, it simulates DR mechanisms as derived through: (i) considering Hourly Electricity Prices (HEPs) and a Limiting Price (LP), and (ii) a more “real-world” situation, in which a central planner attempts to maximize flexibility value by issuing DR signals. This planner learns the optimal policy that maximizes its revenues through an optimization approach based on Reinforcement Learning theory.

Energy Efficiency policies

DREEM can be used to examine different energy efficiency measures and retrofit packages, and behavioral analysis of end-use in the building sector. The model considers different support policy schemes, as subsidies, Pay-for-Performance, etc.

Combinations and beyond 'perfect foresight' modelling

AIM can exploratory analyze simulation outputs under different input assumptions of simulation models (e.g., different support policies for the promotion of RES and storage, diverse socio-economic contexts), to generate policy pathways comprising of policy sequences that not only lead to the desired policy outcomes (e.g. RES integration), but also consider uncertainties in modelling assumptions as potential future evolutions, adding the element of robustness to policy evaluation.

Implications for other SDGs

The TEEM Suite as whole can provide implications for:

      - §7. Affordable and clean energy by providing:
          > the renewable energy share in electricity mix
          > the system marginal price of electricity
          > cost-benefit ratios of decentralized RES generation and Self-consumption
      - §11: Sustainable Cities & Communities by providing:
          > the Levelized Cost of Saved Energy (LCSE) of different Energy Efficiency Measures in the building sector, as well as cost-benefit ratios of Demand-Flexibility
          - $13: Climate action by providing:
            > the CO2 emissions from electricity generation
            > the footprint impact of private consumption in the building sector

Recent use cases

Paper DOI Paper Title Key findings
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.eist.2018.12.004 Barriers to and consequences of a solar-based energy transition in Greece

BSAM simulation results showed that increasing self-consumption will increase, during some months of the year, the price that everybody else pays for electricity, in order for generators to counterbalance their revenue losses. These findings validate literature insights and signal a consequential risk that must be in-corporated into any policy aiming to support self-consumption in Greece.

However, since simulation results were derived by allowing small-scale PV and electricity storage to coordinate with the sole goal ofincreasing self-consumption, it is concluded that just supporting increased self-consumption is suboptimal—at least if optimalitymeans decreasing the price that consumers without self-consumption must pay. To overcome this sub-optimality, policymakingshould trigger self-consumption by measures that have a broader view of the system's state. This entails supporting smart self-consumption,i.e., self-consumption that is triggered by the power system’s needs and activated based on price signals. Mixes of policy measures to support self-consumption of (PV-produced electricity must focus on increasing its profitability and financial appeal to the consumers. Sustainable practices to this end can result from parameterising direct support to PV investments, electricity storage and self-consumption (€/MWh); purchase price of, and tax and levies for, electricity fed to the grid; and grid fees. However, there is a constant need to augment such measures with the infrastructure and regulation necessary to control and steer distributed generation according to the power system’s needs.

Finally, by expecting significant technological breakthroughs in electricity storage manufacturing in the coming years, it becomes apparent that investing in a self-consumption with storage scheme in Greece should be evaluated together with expectedly decreasing costs for storage and the fact that PV self-consumption will balance the frequency and magnitude of peak generation events stressing the distribution network.Thus,it is essential that policymaking focus on new business models that will monetise the value of self-consumption with storage, and on regulatory frameworks that will envisage appropriate technology subsidies and extra provisions to operators, allowing fair allocation of benefits among all actors involved.
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.apenergy.2019.113795 An agent-based model to simulate technology adoption quantifying behavioural uncertainty of consumers

Simulation results showed that the net-metering scheme, as currently stands, is not capable of achieving the necessary capacity addition towards the Greek target of 2025. In particular, while the scheme could be successful until the end of 2021, it won’t be able to reach the national target of 2025, even in the most optimistic scenarios. The effectiveness of the net-metering scheme in Greece is closely related to the retail price of electricity. Simulation results indicated that an increase in the retail price, reduces the total uncertainty in the modeling outcomes, which is mainly introduced by modeling parameters, as the probability of investing, consumers’ beliefs and resistance towards investing. In policy terms, this is translated into a reduction of the behavioural uncertainty related to the effectiveness of the NEM scheme, especially for the case of risk-averse consumers, which implies that different sources of positive financial outcomes for consumers should be further investigated.
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.enconman.2019.112339 A modular high-resolution demand-side management model to quantify benefits of demand-flexibility in the residential sector

Simulation results showed that promoting a synergistic co-operation between the power supplier and the prosumer could lead to significan cost reductions and energy savings, paving the way towards “gamechanger” business models that capture new value on the supply side by coupling it to the demand side.

Policymaking in Greece should focus on promoting small-scale PV with technologies, as battery storage or smart thermostats. Considering the existing ambiguity of the PV market in Greece, such an infrastructure could generate additional sources of revenue for consumers, which could counterbalance the phase out of the previous Feed-in-Tariffs (FiTs) scheme. In particular, policy efforts should focus on more marketbased structures by avoiding cross-subsidization, along with appropriate promotion campaigns that will inform consumers on the benefits of self-consumption, and its influence on their electricity bill. This should be evaluated along with the fact that electricity storage manufacturers are promising revolutionary progress regarding cost reductions in the coming years, and that PV self-consumption can balance the frequency and magnitude of peak generation events that stress the distribution network.
10.1016/j.enpol.2020.111350 A transdisciplinary modeling framework for the participatory design of dynamic adaptive policy pathways Net-metering is capable of driving adequate investments in small-scale PV that follow the Greek PV targets’ trajectory only in the short term. This is because grid charges for net-metered electricity burden the prosumers instead of being used to increase the value of locally generated electricity for their benefit. Thus, bill savings (i.e., prosumers’ profits) are solely dependent on the electricity retail price, which reduces consumers’ willingness to install a photovoltaic system. A self-consumption scheme that subsidizes battery storage showed great potential to incentivise investments y consumers. This is because, with a self-consumption scheme prosumers consume more self-generated electricity since it can be stored for later direct use. Furthermore, grid charges are paid only for the electricity consumed from the grid. As such, there is less price-related uncertainty and prosumers’ profits are higher. However, stepwise reduction of subsidy is suggested, to ensure an initial uptake of PV, wihle avoiding excessive subsidies' cost. Regarding the applicability of AIM, the study showed that while 100% success of a policy pathway, regardless the contextual evolution, cannot be achieved, opportunities and dead-ends can be highlighted and visualized in a stakeholder-friendly fashion.
