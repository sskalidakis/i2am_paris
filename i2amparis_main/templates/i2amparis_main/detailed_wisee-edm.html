The WISEE-EDM model

Short overview

The industrial module of WISEE-EDM is used to analyse possible futures of an industrial production system and to derive technically consistent paths to them, starting from the current production system.

    Typical questions to the model are, for example:
  • Which technology mixes make it possible to meet CO2 emission reduction targets in 2050?
  • When do investments in innovative industrial processes have to start in order to achieve a set target status while maintaining the usual reinvestment cycles?
  • Which energy sources are needed for a target-oriented path, in what quantities, when and where?
  • What will future production networks look like in technological and geographical terms?

The model has a high level of technical detail and offers a variety of options for adapting the analysis to scenario-specific assumptions. It is therefore particularly suitable for developing scenarios in exchange with stakeholders.

    The industrial module of WISEE-EDM is a "bottom-up" model of industrial production. Bottom-up means that aggregated values such as CO2 emissions of the German steel industry are derived from the properties and operation of the individual plants covered by the model. Its databasis of industry plants currently (2020) covers the following industries:
  • for EU-27+3: iron & steel, basic chemicals, refineries
  • for Germany in addition: pulp & paper, non-ferrous metals, cement, lime, glass (more on this see â€žKey featuresâ€œ).
Developments of energy demand and CO2-emissions in other sectors are calculated using econometric methods.

    The industrial module of WISEE-EDM is strictly speaking a model family whose central components are the following two modules:
  • WISEE-EDM Dispatch (EDM-D): Calculates the use (dispatch) of production plants and the associated CO2 emissions, energy requirements, feedstock flows and the production of by-products (e.g. hydrogen from chlor-alkali electrolysis).
  • WISEE-EDM-Invest (EDM-I): Optimizes the Europe-wide investments in production facilities from an economic point of view over a given period of time, taking into account the demand for end products and transport costs between locations. (Currently parameterized for the chemical industry only)

The model results cover selected years (typically 5-year steps) within the specified simulated time frame (e.g. 2020 - 2050). While the invest module optimizes over the whole time frame, the dispatch model calculates results for each selected year in turn.

Key features of the WISEE-EDM model

High level of technological detail

    Energy requirements and CO2 emissions are calculated based on the (assumed) physical production of goods. The database of the WISEE-EDM industry module includes:
  • Approx. 800 production sites in Europe. These are covered with GIS data and assigned to the EU member states and various NUTS levels (NUTS 1, NUTS 3). The database also contains the connection of sites of the chemical industry and refineries by pipelines. Furthermore, the (future) connections of sites to a CO2 or hydrogen infrastructure can be specified (scenario-dependent).
  • Approx. 200 technologies (e.g. blast furnace, steam cracker, rotary kiln) with their respective specific energy requirements (differentiated by 25 energy carriers), process-related emissions, selected material inputs and typical technical lifetimes. Technologies on the European average standard of the years 2000 to 2005 as well as the currently best available technology (BAT) and assumed future processes (e.g. reduction of iron ore by means of hydrogen) are included.
  • > 2600 industrial production plants. The production plants are specified in terms of the technology used (see above) and their production capacity. For particularly energy-intensive plants, the year in which they were built is also specified. All production facilities are assigned to the respective sites.
  • > 250 industrial power plants, specified by fuel, maximum capacity (electrical, thermal), operating strategy and year of construction. They are assigned to the respective sites.

Consideration of industrial co-products

The production of steam, steel gases and hydrogen as by-products of industrial production is accounted for at each site or within the inter-site networks defined in the model, and is taken into account in the calculation of (remaining) energy requirements and the operation of industrial power plants. For the production of steel and basic chemicals, balances are also prepared for selected quantities of materials (educts and products).

Consideration of production chains

The investment module is characterized by a consideration of complex production chains in connection with transport possibilities and costs of intermediate products.

Climate module & emissions granularity

CO2 emissions are calculated based on the (assumed) physical production of goods considering the site-specific efficiency of applied technologies and the choice of energy carriers as well as the use of carbon capture technologies (if any). CO2 emissions are calculated for each production plant and can be aggregated to NUTS 3-, NUTS 1-, national and European levels.

Socioeconomic dimensions

Production levels

Production levels are a main driver of the model. Economic growth is a main input variable to the definition of production level scenarios used by the model. Furthermore, effects of behavioural changes on production levels (e.g. less demand for cars) can be included in the specification of production level scenarios. The fine-grained differentiation of products by the model allows for a detailed representation of developments in the various industrial sectors.

Impacts of political and societal discourses

The model is flexible to include impacts of politial and societal discourses on the industrial production system such as the future (non-)availability of infractures (e.g. CO2-infrastructure) and technologies.

Mitigation/adaptation measures and technologies

    The model is capable of including technical mitigation measures such as:
  • Efficiency of industrial production technologies
  • Innovative production technologies (e.g. DRI for steel making)
  • Fuel switches
  • Carbon capture and storage
  • Changes to production levels of particular goods

The model does not include adaptation measures.

Economic rationale and model solution

The core principle behind the investment related part of the model (EDM-I) is that of societal costs of industrial production of a given amount of products. EDM-I is an optimization model that minimizes the investments and (simplified) operation costs over the whole simulated time period (while considering the value of assets at the end of the time period).

The dispatch part of the model (EDM-D) does not involve economic considerations but is a purely technical model. It calculates energy demand and emissions based on production levels and characteristics of the production plants operative in the selected year according to the scenario.

Key parameters

Demand for products / Production levels

The central input variables from which the dispatch in the EDM-D is calculated on the assumption of an even utilization of all plants are the production capacities available in the selected year and the production quantities. The production levels and production capacities can either be directly set in the EDM-D or be provided by the EDM-I (for the chemical industry). For running the EDM-I, the demand for products needs to be specified. Invest costs and operation costs for different technologies as well as transport costs for intermediary products are parameterized in the model but can be adapted.

Technological development

Other central parameters are the years at which innovative production technlogies are assumed to be available on an industrial scale and the speed of market penetration with BAT technology or further improved production processes.

Infrastructure development

The time at which infrastructures (H2, CO2) will be available (in the future) needs to be defined in the model on a site-specific basis per scenario.

Policy questions and SDGs

Key policies that can be addressed

While the WISEE-EDM does not feature a policy interface, it is able to simulate the effects of a range of different types of policies through adaptation of the models parameters:

Energy policy

The invest module (EDM-I) can directly assess the effects of energy related policies that affect prices (tax, subsidies) or availability of energy carriers (e.g. coal exit). In the dispatch module (EDM-D) the standard energy demand associated with the usage of particular production technologies may also be amended in a rule-based manner to cover fuel switches (e.g. coal exit, replacement of natural gas by biomethan etc.).

R&D related policies

The model can assess the effects of R&D related policies by variation of the year from which on technologies are availabe on an industrial scale level. The model may be used to check the influence of such measures on the development of the industrial production stock â€“ depending on the age composition of the plants, availability of infrastructure, and other factors. Furthermore, policies that support the diffusion of innovative technologies can be represented through variation of the replacement rate of standard technologies through best available technologies.

Infrastructure related policies

The model can assess the effects of infrastructure related policies and regulations through variation of industry sitesâ€™ connection to infrastructures.

Implications for other SDGs

WISEE-EDM does not automatically calculate the implications on non-climate SDGs. However, it is possible to use its outputs and calculate the predictions for certain indicators framed in the SDG agenda.

Simulating geographically distributed production networks of a climate neutral European petrochemical industry The paper describes quantitative scenarios on a possible evolution of the EU petrochemical industry towards climate neutrality. The cost-optimization model used to develop the scenarios describes at which sites investments of industry in the production stock could take place in the future. Around 50 types of products, the related production processes and the respective sites have been collected in a database. The processes included cover the production chain from platform chemicals via intermediates to polymers. Pipelines allowing for efficient exchange of feedstock and platform chemicals between sites are taken into account as well. The model draws on this data to simulate capacity change at individual plants as well as plant utilization. Thus, a future European production network for petrochemicals with flows between the different sites and steps of the value chain can be sketched. The scenarios described in this paper reveal how an electrification strategy could be implemented by European industry over time with minimized societal costs.
